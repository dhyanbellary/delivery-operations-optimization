Delivery Operations Optimization

I built this project to understand where time actually gets lost in a food delivery system. Using a simulated dataset of 250 orders modeled on real-world platforms like Swiggy and Zomato, I broke down each stage of the delivery lifecycle prep, rider assignment, pickup, and transit to identify bottlenecks using data instead of assumptions. The goal was to move from observation to measurable insights and operational decisions.

Key Findings

  • [Finding 1: Transit consumes the largest share of delivery time, averaging 32.3 minutes out of 57 minutes (~56% of total time).]
  • [Finding 2: Peak hours increase delivery time by ~10% due to demand spikes and reduced rider availability.]
  • [Finding 3: Bulk orders take ~41% longer, driven primarily by higher kitchen prep time, not transit delays.]
  • [Finding 4: Distance is a stronger driver of delivery time than traffic level, making route length a key factor.]
  • [Finding 5: The highest failure rate occurs during the evening (4-6 PM), indicating early system stress before peak hours.]
  • [Finding 6: Rider performance varies significantly, with up to ~15-minute difference in average transit time across riders.]
  • [Finding 7: 7 PM is the operational bottleneck, with highest order volume and lowest rider availability occurring simultaneously.]
  • [Finding 8: Short distance orders still take ~43 minutes, showing prep and wait time dominate over travel time.]

Recommendations

  • [Recommendation 1: Increase rider availability between 5:30-8:30 PM to reduce the peak hour supply-demand gap.]
  • [Recommendation 2: Implement kitchen SLA monitoring for bulk orders to reduce excessive prep time delays.]
  • [Recommendation 3: Pre-assign riders before order readiness to minimize pickup wait time and idle gaps.]
  • [Recommendation 4: Monitor and optimize low-performing riders through better routing and allocation strategies.]
